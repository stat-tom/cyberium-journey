# Lesson 10 - Attack and Incident Response Plans

## Status
- Not completed

## Why Response Planning Matters
- Preventive controls will not stop every incident.
- A clear incident response plan reduces confusion, speeds up containment, and lowers business impact.

## Incident Response Lifecycle
- Preparation: define tools, roles, access, logging, contacts, and playbooks before an incident occurs.
- Identification: confirm whether suspicious activity is a real incident and determine its scope.
- Containment: limit the spread and protect critical assets.
- Eradication: remove malware, close the root cause, and eliminate attacker persistence.
- Recovery: restore systems safely and monitor for recurrence.
- Lessons learned: document what happened and improve controls and procedures.

## What a Good Plan Includes
- Roles and responsibilities, including technical owners, management, legal, communications, and external contacts.
- Severity levels and escalation criteria.
- Communication channels for internal and external stakeholders.
- Evidence handling guidance to preserve logs and forensic value.
- Playbooks for common scenarios such as ransomware, phishing, account compromise, and data leakage.

## Attack Planning From the Defender Perspective
- Security teams also prepare for probable attack paths by running tabletop exercises, simulations, and detection engineering.
- This improves readiness before a real attacker forces fast decisions.

## Common Mistakes
- No clear owner for containment decisions.
- No tested backup restoration process.
- Missing contact lists or inaccessible documentation during an outage.
- Failure to collect evidence early.
- Weak post-incident review, causing the same issue to repeat.

## Notes
- A plan that has never been practiced is only partially useful. Exercises and simulations are essential.
