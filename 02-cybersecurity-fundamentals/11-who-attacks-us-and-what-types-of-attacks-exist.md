# Lesson 11 - Who Attacks Us and What Types of Attacks Exist?

## Common Threat Actors
- Cybercriminals seek financial gain through fraud, extortion, theft, and resale of access or data.
- State-sponsored groups pursue espionage, disruption, political influence, or strategic advantage.
- Hacktivists attack to promote political or social causes.
- Insiders may cause harm deliberately or by negligence.
- Competitors or contractors may abuse trusted access in some environments.
- Opportunistic attackers scan the internet for easy targets, regardless of company size.

## Common Attack Types
- Phishing and social engineering trick users into revealing credentials or executing malicious actions.
- Malware includes trojans, worms, spyware, botnet agents, and ransomware.
- Credential attacks include password spraying, brute force, credential stuffing, and session theft.
- Network attacks include denial of service, man-in-the-middle, rogue access points, and protocol abuse.
- Web attacks include SQL injection, cross-site scripting, authentication bypass, and insecure deserialization.
- Supply chain attacks target vendors, dependencies, or update mechanisms.
- Insider attacks involve misuse of authorized access.

## How Attackers Choose Targets
- Exposure: systems reachable from the internet are easier to find.
- Value: sensitive data, payment systems, and privileged infrastructure are attractive.
- Weakness: unpatched software, weak passwords, and poor monitoring lower the attack cost.
- Timing: attackers often exploit mergers, product launches, crises, holidays, or known incidents.

## Defender Takeaways
- Not every attacker has the same skill, budget, or objective.
- Security controls should be prioritized based on realistic adversaries and the assets they want.
- User awareness, hardening, monitoring, and response readiness all matter because attackers use different paths to the same goal.

## Notes
- Understanding the attacker helps translate generic security advice into practical priorities.
