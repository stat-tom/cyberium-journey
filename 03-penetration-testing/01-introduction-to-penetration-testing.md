# Lesson 1 - Introduction to Penetration Testing

## Status
- Planned

## Definition
- Penetration testing is an authorized security assessment in which a tester simulates realistic attack paths to identify weaknesses in systems, applications, networks, or processes.
- The goal is not just to find vulnerabilities, but to show how they could be abused and what impact they could have on the organization.

## Main Objectives
- Discover exploitable weaknesses before real attackers do.
- Validate whether security controls work in practice, not only on paper.
- Measure the business impact of chained vulnerabilities.
- Provide prioritized remediation guidance based on real attack paths.

## What a Penetration Test Is Not
- It is not random hacking or activity without permission.
- It is not the same as a vulnerability scan, because a penetration test includes manual validation and exploitation where allowed.
- It is not a guarantee that every issue in the environment will be found.

## Common Types of Penetration Tests
- Web application tests focus on issues such as broken access control, injection, authentication flaws, and insecure business logic.
- Network tests assess internal or external infrastructure, exposed services, segmentation, and weak configurations.
- Wireless tests evaluate Wi-Fi security, rogue access points, and weak authentication setups.
- Social engineering tests examine how people and processes respond to phishing, impersonation, or pretexting.
- Red team exercises are broader adversary simulations focused on realistic goals and detection capabilities.

## Typical Deliverables
- An executive summary describing business risk in plain language.
- Technical findings with evidence, affected assets, attack paths, and remediation guidance.
- A severity assessment to help prioritize fixes.
- Recommendations for improving controls, monitoring, and hardening.

## Notes
- A useful penetration test is scoped, authorized, evidence-based, and tied to remediation, not just exploitation.
